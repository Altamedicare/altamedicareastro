How Medicare Penalties Work

Part B penalty

Your Part B premium increases 10% for each full 12-month period you could have had Part B but didn't enroll. In most cases you pay it for as long as you have Part B.

Part D penalty

1% of the national base premium ($38.99 in 2026) for every full month you went without Part D or other creditable drug coverage — added to your premium for life and recalculated as the base changes.

How to avoid them

Enroll when first eligible, or keep creditable coverage (employer, union, VA) and enroll during your Special Enrollment Period when that coverage ends. Avoid gaps of 63+ days for drug coverage.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Part B late enrollment penalty?
Your Part B premium goes up 10% for each full 12-month period you could have had Part B but didn't sign up. In most cases you pay it for as long as you have Part B.
What is the Part D late enrollment penalty?
It's 1% of the national base beneficiary premium ($38.99 in 2026) times the number of full months you went without Part D or other creditable drug coverage, added to your premium for as long as you have Part D.
How do I avoid Medicare late enrollment penalties?
Enroll when first eligible, or keep creditable coverage (employer, union, VA) and enroll within your Special Enrollment Period when it ends.
Are the penalties permanent?
In most cases yes — they're added to your monthly premiums for as long as you have that coverage.
What counts as creditable coverage?
Coverage at least as good as Medicare's, such as an active employer group plan, union, or VA benefits.
Do the penalty amounts change each year?
Yes — they're based on the standard Part B premium and the Part D national base premium, which are set annually.
